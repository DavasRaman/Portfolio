About Me

Jack of all trades,
thread of one ocean.

I am a final-year Bachelor of Marine Science (Marine Ecology) student at Griffith University, originally from Labasa, Vanua Levu, Fiji, and based on the Gold Coast. I have spent the better part of my life moving between two worlds: the coral reefs and mangrove coastlines of Fiji that shaped who I am, and the academic and professional environment of Queensland that has given me the tools to do something about what I care about.

I am a recreational freediver, a bush restoration worker, a coral reef researcher, and a GIS mapping specialist. My experience spans cetacean biological sampling across two humpback whale seasons with SOPOPP, conservation volunteering at Elephant Nature Park in Thailand, independent orangutan reforestation along the Kinabatangan River in Borneo, and ongoing habitat restoration work across south-east Queensland with OzFish Unlimited as a Young Leader and Volunteer Habitat Officer.

I am not the kind of person who specialises in one thing and stays there. I am most useful at the edges: where ecology meets community, where GIS meets fieldwork, and where Fiji meets Queensland. My long-term goal is to return to Labasa and contribute to community-based coastal restoration combining mangrove rehabilitation, blue carbon, fisheries linkages, and sustainable aquaculture in partnership with villages holding customary coastal rights.

Synopsis

OzFish Unlimited is a national conservation organisation dedicated to restoring and protecting Australia's recreational fishing environments through community-led habitat restoration. The Gold Coast chapter operates across key estuarine and coastal systems on Yugambeh Country, targeting degraded habitats through mangrove replanting, oyster reef construction, tangle bin monitoring, and community education: interventions increasingly recognised as critical to the recovery of fish nursery habitats in modified urban waterways (Barbier et al., 2011).

As an OzFish Young Leader, I completed 150 hours across a range of roles under the supervision of Erica Leal and Robbie Porter. The majority of my time was devoted to geospatial analysis and mapping, producing QGIS-based restoration site maps across multiple sites throughout Queensland, covering mangrove rehabilitation extents, oyster and fish habitat restoration zones, and print-ready map exports formatted for government and funding proposals. Sites spanned northern and south-east Queensland, including Eprapah Creek and a range of locations assessed as candidates for future oyster reef restoration, supporting both local chapter operations and broader regional planning. In addition, I proposed and began implementing an ArcGIS Online system for the SEQ team, designed to enable live data collection by volunteers in the field via Field Maps. This initiative has since gained traction and I am now leading its continued development and rollout within the chapter. I also coordinated and participated in community working bees, contributing to habitat restoration activities on the ground and helping to build volunteer capacity within the chapter. Additional fieldwork included a fish kill survey at Mudgeeraba Creek, assessing water quality and species impact following an acute pollution event, and fish surveys conducted to support ongoing habitat assessment for a fish later initiative.

Critical Reflection

This placement significantly deepened my practical proficiency in GIS. Prior to OzFish, I had developed foundational QGIS skills through university coursework, but working within a real conservation organisation required me to produce outputs that met professional standards: accurate, print-ready, and immediately usable by non-GIS staff and stakeholders. Producing restoration maps across Queensland taught me to think critically about symbology, scale, and data communication in a way that academic exercises rarely demand. Proposing and beginning to implement the ArcGIS Online system pushed me further still. I had to understand not only the technical capabilities of the platform but how it could realistically be integrated into a volunteer-run organisation with limited resources and variable digital literacy.

Beyond the technical, this placement gave me a much clearer understanding of how NGOs function and why community engagement is not peripheral to conservation. It is the mechanism. I observed firsthand that habitat restoration at the scale OzFish operates depends entirely on volunteer buy-in, local trust, and sustained community participation. Working bees were as much about building relationships as they were about planting mangroves. This shifted how I think about my own long-term ambitions in coastal restoration in Fiji: technical knowledge is necessary but insufficient without the community structures to sustain it.

My degree supported this experience in meaningful ways. Estuarine ecology coursework gave me the conceptual grounding to contextualise what I was mapping. Understanding why Eprapah Creek's mangrove fringe matters for juvenile fish recruitment, or what a fish kill event signals about catchment health, made me a more effective contributor than a GIS technician alone could be. Moving forward, I intend to pursue a pathway into coastal and estuarine research, ideally through Honours and then a PhD, building on the applied skills and professional relationships this placement has established.
